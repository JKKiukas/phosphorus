The coastal waters and open sea areas of Finland have almost entirely become eutrophied due to excessive nutrient loading. Phosphorus is a valuable nutrient in primary production, but in excessive amounts, it causes eutrophication and oxygen depletion in water bodies. Phosphorus reaches the sea through erosion, for example, via rivers. In the Archipelago Sea, two factors—agriculture and natural runoff—constitute a total of 86% of the total phosphorus load ending up in the archipelago sea. Total phosphorus concentration indicates the total amount of phosphorus in the water, which is an important indicator for assessing water eutrophication. The unit of measurement is µg/l (micrograms per liter), and 1 mg = 1000 µg. The eutrophication classification based on total phosphorus concentration, as defined by the Water Administration, is as follows: <12 = barren, blue 12-30 = slightly eutrophic, green 30-50 = eutrophic, yellow 50-100 = highly eutrophic, orange >100 = excessively eutrophic, red As climate change progresses, mild and wet autumns and winters are becoming more common, and it is believed that erosion and the amount of nutrient loading will also increase. There are ways to manage and reduce phosphorus loading. Agricultural nutrient loading can be reduced, for example, through intense autumn plowing and field gypsum treatment.
